# Monetization And Capital

Use this reference for pricing, packaging, subscriptions, revenue models, unit economics,
financial assumptions, and funding strategy.

## Monetization Starts With Value

Map:

- User, buyer, approver, and budget owner
- Valuable outcome and current alternative spend
- Frequency, magnitude, and measurability of value
- Incremental cost and risk to serve
- Segment differences in value and willingness to pay
- The unit that grows when customer value grows

A monetization model is coherent when the value metric, cost structure, customer budget,
and product architecture can scale together.

## Pricing Workflow

### 1. Segment by value and behavior

Do not package only by company size. Segment by job, urgency, workflow complexity, risk,
volume, collaboration, service burden, and realized value.

### 2. Choose a value metric

Evaluate candidates such as seats, active users, usage, transactions, records, managed
assets, locations, workflow volume, outcome, flat access, or a hybrid.

A strong value metric is:

- Legible and forecastable to the buyer
- Difficult to game
- Correlated with realized value
- Compatible with gross margin and technical metering
- Able to expand without turning successful usage into punishment

### 3. Package meaningful differences

Tiers should represent distinct customer situations, service levels, risk, control, or
workflow depth. Do not create an intentionally bad decoy or hold basic safety, export,
privacy, and accessibility hostage to a premium plan.

### 4. Set and test prices

Use evidence in this order:

1. Consequential choices: purchases, renewals, deposits, preorders, paid pilots, upgrades
2. Existing spend, replacement budget, and switching behavior
3. Controlled price or packaging experiments with guardrails
4. Competitive prices adjusted for segment, scope, and value metric
5. Structured willingness-to-pay research, such as Gabor-Granger or conjoint
6. Interviews and open-ended surveys as hypothesis input

Hypothetical answers can overstate real willingness to pay. Do not infer a precise price
from compliments, a tiny convenience sample, or competitor anchors.

Anchoring, price endings, and decoy effects may change choice presentation, but they cannot
repair a weak value metric or missing willingness to pay. Use transparent comparison and
test effects in the actual segment.

## Subscription Fit

Do not default to a subscription because the product is software. A subscription needs
recurring or accumulating value at a credible cadence.

Evaluate alternatives:

- Subscription for continuous access, workflow, monitoring, or stored value
- Usage-based pricing when variable consumption tracks value and cost
- Transaction or take rate when the product enables an exchange
- License or one-time payment for durable local utility
- Services for expertise and labor, priced honestly as services
- Outcome-based pricing when outcomes are measurable and attribution is credible
- Hybrid models when fixed availability and variable value both matter

For subscriptions, inspect activation, cohort retention, voluntary and involuntary churn,
expansion, contraction, payment recovery, gross margin, support burden, and natural usage
frequency. Do not use cancellation obstruction as a retention strategy.

## Unit Economics

Use formulas only when inputs are stable enough to support them. Show assumptions and
sensitivity rather than false precision.

```text
gross margin = (revenue - variable cost to serve) / revenue
CAC payback months = acquisition cost / monthly gross profit from a new customer
simple subscription LTV ~= ARPA * gross margin / logo churn
```

The simple LTV approximation is unreliable with small cohorts, changing churn, material
expansion, annual contracts, heterogeneous segments, or usage-based revenue. Prefer cohort
cash flows and contribution margin when data exists.

Check:

- Does price support the acquisition and service motion?
- Is sales complexity appropriate for contract value?
- Does growth improve or degrade margin and support load?
- Are discounts buying duration, volume, evidence, or merely hiding weak demand?
- Which assumption most changes runway or payback?

## Capital Strategy

Funding is a means to a milestone, not validation or a default badge of ambition.

Frame the decision with:

- Milestone that capital unlocks
- Amount, timing, runway, and contingency required
- Whether money accelerates a known engine or subsidizes unresolved demand
- Market speed and cost of waiting
- Return profile and likely investor expectations
- Dilution, control, covenants, repayment, and distraction
- Credible next financing or path to sustainability

Compare:

| Source | Best Fit | Core Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Customer revenue | Early value can be sold and growth can follow cash | Slower learning or customer concentration |
| Bootstrapping | Modest capital need, early revenue, control matters | Opportunity cost and founder capacity |
| Grants | Research, public benefit, deep tech, or eligible programs | Restricted use and application time |
| Debt or revenue finance | Predictable cash flow and bounded use of funds | Repayment pressure and covenants |
| Angels | Early uncertainty where expertise and network matter | Fragmented ownership and mixed incentives |
| Venture capital | Outlier-scale outcome, speed advantage, large market, capital-accelerated engine | Dilution, power-law expectations, pressure to scale |
| Strategic capital | Distribution, supply, data, or market access is central | Dependency and constrained strategic options |

Do not recommend venture funding solely because competitors raised money. Venture fit
requires a plausible outlier return and a reason rapid capital deployment improves the
odds. A profitable niche product can be excellent and still be a poor venture investment.

Express an ask as a milestone model:

```text
capital -> planned capabilities and experiments -> measurable milestone ->
decision or financing options created
```

State what must be true for the amount and timeline to work. Do not invent hockey-stick
forecasts to make the story fundable.

## Monetization Tests

Every proposal should include a consequential test where feasible:

| Question | Stronger Test |
|---|---|
| Does the segment value this? | Paid pilot, deposit, preorder, or signed budget-backed LOI |
| Which package fits? | Choice test with realistic tradeoffs and follow-up interviews |
| Is the value metric acceptable? | Quote or invoice simulation using the customer's real volume |
| Can price rise? | Controlled offer across comparable qualified cohorts |
| Should this be a subscription? | Measure recurring trigger, repeat value, and cohort retention |
| Should the company raise? | Milestone/runway scenarios and evidence that spend accelerates a proven constraint |

Protect trust, fairness, refund rates, support load, and long-term retention as guardrails.
