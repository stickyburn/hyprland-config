# Evidence And Markets

Use this reference for market selection, customer discovery, competitive analysis,
distribution, demand validation, and historical analogies.

## Build an Evidence Packet

Gather only what can change the current decision. Start with this hierarchy:

1. Product behavior, retained cohorts, transactions, renewals, cancellations, and support data
2. Direct observation of current workflows, purchases, workarounds, and switching behavior
3. Customer interviews about specific past events, not future enthusiasm
4. Primary sources: pricing pages, product docs, filings, regulators, public datasets, changelogs
5. Peer-reviewed research and transparent industry datasets
6. Independent reporting and analyst work with disclosed methodology
7. Reviews, communities, and social posts as qualitative signals, not representative samples
8. Vendor blogs, SEO pages, unsourced market reports, and model memory as leads only

Do not count syndicated copies or several articles citing one report as independent
corroboration. Preserve contradictions; they often reveal segment or definition differences.

## Research Streams

Run the smallest relevant set of streams. Parallelize only independent work.

### Demand And Behavior

Look for:

- Trigger events and frequency of the problem
- Existing spend, staff time, risk, delay, or workarounds
- Search behavior, requests for recommendations, migration stories, and cancellation reasons
- Who experiences the pain, who approves a purchase, and who owns implementation
- Evidence of non-consumption and why people tolerate the status quo

Treat reviews and forums as biased samples. They are strong for vocabulary, edge cases,
and hypothesis generation, weak for prevalence estimates.

For interviews, ask for the last concrete occurrence:

- "Walk me through the last time this happened."
- "What did you do next?"
- "What did that cost in time, money, delay, or risk?"
- "What have you already tried?"
- "Who else became involved?"
- "What made you keep the current solution?"

Avoid pitching, leading questions, and hypothetical willingness to pay.

### Alternatives And Competition

Map the full choice set, not just direct logos:

- Direct products
- Broader suites with a good-enough feature
- Spreadsheets, agencies, consultants, internal tools, and manual labor
- Delay, risk acceptance, and doing nothing
- A different budget category competing for the same money

For each important alternative, capture target segment, core job, value metric, price and
packaging, acquisition motion, strengths, complaints, switching costs, strategic assets,
and recent direction. Verify capabilities through documentation or the product when
possible; a comparison page is not neutral evidence.

Look for asymmetries rather than empty feature cells:

- A segment incumbents cannot serve without hurting margins or positioning
- A workflow their architecture makes expensive
- A channel their sales model cannot exploit
- A trust, privacy, latency, deployment, or ownership requirement they cannot credibly meet
- A value metric that better aligns price with realized value

### Market Economics And Distribution

Estimate a reachable market from the bottom up:

```text
reachable accounts or users
x realistic annual value per account or user
x serviceable share within the chosen channel and time horizon
= decision-relevant market
```

Use top-down reports only as a definition check or outer bound. Explicitly state the
population, geography, price, adoption, and share assumptions. A large TAM does not prove
urgency, access, retention, or willingness to pay.

Map distribution before assuming the product will "go viral":

- Where does the trigger event happen?
- Where do users currently seek help or compare alternatives?
- Which party already has trusted access to the buyer?
- Is the channel repeatable, measurable, and economically compatible with price and margin?
- Does product use create an artifact, collaboration, integration, result, or reputation that
  naturally reaches another qualified user?

## Historical And Cross-Market Analogies

Search for mechanisms, not a list of famous companies. Useful searches combine the target
constraint with patterns such as referral economics, marketplace liquidity, category entry,
bottom-up adoption, unbundling, failed launches, pricing migration, and shutdown postmortems.

Record each analogy as:

| Field | Required Analysis |
|---|---|
| Case | Company/product, geography, customer, and exact period |
| Outcome | What changed, with a primary or credible source |
| Mechanism | Why the tactic plausibly changed behavior or economics |
| Conditions | Channel, competition, technology, norms, regulation, and subsidy |
| Target mapping | Structural relation shared with the current problem |
| Transfer limits | Conditions that differ or causal evidence that is missing |
| Adaptation | Smallest modern version worth testing |

Founder interviews and retrospective blog posts are evidence about the operator's model,
not proof that one tactic caused the outcome. Seek contemporary data, experiments, filings,
or independent accounts when the causal claim matters.

## Synthesis

Use an evidence table for material claims:

| Claim | Label | Best Evidence | Contradiction | Confidence | Decision Impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Then extract:

- Strongest demand signal
- Strongest status-quo force
- Most important underserved behavior or segment
- Distribution opening
- Economic constraint
- Timing reason
- Fatal unknown

Stop researching when more information is unlikely to change the next decision. If the
fatal unknown remains, recommend a test or data request instead of manufacturing certainty.

## Research Basis

- The 40% product-market-fit survey threshold is a practitioner heuristic, not a universal
  proof. Pair survey sentiment with segment-level retention, repeat behavior, referrals, and
  willingness to pay.
- Hypothetical willingness-to-pay answers commonly differ from consequential choices.
  Prefer transactions and behavior whenever feasible.
- Current market facts decay quickly. Date sources and use live research instead of values
  embedded in this skill.
